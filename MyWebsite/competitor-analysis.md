# Market Intelligence Report
## Soul Waves — Competitive Landscape Analysis
**Prepared:** May 2026 | **Analyst:** Website Intelligence Engine (Firecrawl + CLAUDE.md)

---

## Executive Summary

Eight sites were scraped and analysed against Soul Waves (Anna Schuster, soulwavesva.com). The market splits into two tribes:

1. **Kajabi-specialist service agencies** — Kirsty Spain (KS-VA), Charlotte Goss, Onit Virtual Services, MB Virtual Solutions, Funnel Pandit, River City VA — compete primarily on platform expertise, volume of features, and social proof.
2. **Premium solo freelancers** — Constance Souville (frontend dev), Nick Stepuk (design studio) — compete on craft, taste, and identity, not on feature lists.

Soul Waves sits at a unique, currently unclaimed intersection: a solo operator with a real backstory and broad digital skill (websites, Shopify, chatbots, automations) who could position like the premium freelancers AND serve the market the agency players target. The gap is: she is not yet presenting like the person at that intersection.

River City VA was offline (Bluehost account suspended) — flagged as a defunct/exiting competitor. One less credible competitor in the space.

## Patterns of the Top 10% in This Space

### 1. Work Over Words
Kirsty Spain has named case studies. Charlotte Goss links to live client builds. Constance Souville and Nick Stepuk show nothing but work. The bottom performers describe services in paragraphs. The market has trained buyers to discount description and trust demonstration.

### 2. One Clear Primary Offer
Charlotte Goss has one retainer. Kirsty Spain has three services but leads with one. Constance has one way to contact. The sites with four or more competing CTAs fail the 5-second test. Fewer doors equal more conversions.

### 3. Premium Proof Comes from Specificity
"Converted into actual sales in the first few days" (Charlotte Goss) beats "She's amazing!" Generic praise is everywhere. Outcomes with details — even small details like a time frame or metric — land with far more authority.

### 4. Confidence Filters the Right Clients
Constance and Nick do not chase the visitor. Their sites say: this is what I do, these are people who hired me, here is how to reach me. This posture communicates selectivity, which communicates value. Charlotte Goss's "different kind" opener does the same in words. Soul Waves has the story to do this better than all of them.

### 5. The Story IS the Differentiator
No competitor in this set has a backstory that stops scrolling. Kirsty Spain is "a tech enthusiast." Milena is "passionate about working with women entrepreneurs." Charlotte is "a systems studio owner." They are serviceable identities, not unforgettable ones. Anna Schuster is a former prison guard who taught herself HTML in Lombok and now builds digital businesses. That story does not exist anywhere else in this market. It is the ultimate differentiator — and it is currently buried.

## Lessons from Constance Souville and Nick Stepuk

### From Constance Souville
1. Build your own site as the portfolio. Her site is proof before a project is clicked. Anna's site should be the best thing she has built — visually, technically, emotionally.
2. Three sentences of bio. "I used to work in a prison. I taught myself to code in Lombok. Now I build digital back-ends for coaches and consultants." That is the bio. Nothing else needed.
3. Typography as positioning. The letter-spacing, the scroll behavior, the negative space — these communicate premium before anyone reads a word.
4. Let the work answer "why you?" A live portfolio with three real client builds answers the question better than any paragraph of copy.

### From Nick Stepuk
1. Client pull-quotes as scroll anchors. Put "This transformed my business" mid-page, not in a testimonial carousel. Break the page with one big, bold client quote. More readable and more trustworthy than a slide.
2. Video and motion for work presentation. If you built a scroll-animated site for a client, show it in motion. A 5-second screen recording autoplay communicates more than 200 words of description.
3. Studio framing. "Soul Waves" is a studio, a brand, a reputation. Lean into it. Use "we" in brand language even as a solo — implies scale and continuity.
4. "Get in touch" as the only CTA. If the story and work are strong enough, the visitor is already sold before they reach the button. The CTA becomes a formality.

## Top 5 Insights Before We Build Anything

### Insight 1: The Story Must Lead
Every Kajabi VA competitor leads with their service. Anna has a weapon none of them have: a story that makes people stop. The prison guard → HTML → Lombok arc is marketing gold and it is sitting on the About page where no one finds it unless they are already interested. Move the hook to the hero. Even one sentence: "I used to work in a prison. Now I build the digital back-end of your business." This creates curiosity, credibility, and contrast in the same breath — before a visitor has scrolled an inch.

### Insight 2: A Portfolio of Work Beats a List of Services
KS-VA has 6 named case studies with real outcomes. Charlotte Goss links to live client builds. Constance Souville IS her portfolio. Anna lists four service categories without showing what they actually look like. The single highest-leverage improvement for conversions: build 3-5 real portfolio items with screenshots, client type, tools used, and one outcome metric. Even rough, early work with permission to show it outperforms a list of capabilities every time.

### Insight 3: The German-Speaking Market is Completely Uncontested
Zero competitors in this set offer bilingual EN/DE service or target the DACH market for Kajabi VA or digital product support. Anna's Austrian background, German fluency, formal "Sie" tone, and DSGVO compliance knowledge is a genuinely defensible niche. A German-language landing page targeting "Kajabi VA für Coaches" or "Online-Kurs-Technik" has almost no competition. This is the clearest blue-ocean opportunity in the data.

### Insight 4: Charlotte Goss's Retainer Architecture is the Right Model to Emulate and Elevate
GBP 800/month for a weekly half-day is a clean, premium-feeling retainer structure. For Soul Waves, a similar architecture (fixed monthly retainer, set hours, defined scope, minimum term) would eliminate the hourly rate conversation, create recurring predictable revenue, signal that Anna's time is limited and valuable, and allow her to go deep with 2-3 clients rather than juggling 10 project requests. The difference from Charlotte: Anna brings a wider scope (Shopify, chatbots, automations — not just Kajabi) and a far better story. The retainer should reflect that premium breadth.

### Insight 5: Soul Waves' Design System is Already Ahead — But the Site Must Prove It
The CLAUDE.md brand system (copper, cream, brown, Great Vibes + Tsukimi Rounded, editorial photography, specific scroll animations) is more sophisticated than anything in this competitor set. The challenge is that the site must visually deliver on that system in a way that makes a coach say "this is the person I want building my platform — because look at theirs." Constance Souville and Nick Stepuk prove that the freelancer's own site is the most powerful portfolio piece they have. Every scroll animation, every image treatment, every micro-interaction on soulwavesva.com must reflect the quality of work Anna promises to deliver. If the site is generic, the promise is unconvincing — regardless of how good the copy is.

1. Hero section that leads with the story hook — not the service list

6. The site itself as craft signal — every animation, every font weight, every image crop must feel intentional
